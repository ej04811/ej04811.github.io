  1. Why did you decide to become a nurse?
    2.
    • There are really 2 reasons I decided on nursing. The first is that after years of sitting behind a desk I felt like I wanted a career where what I did really could make a difference in someone’s life, even if it was only on a small level. The second reason was all of the different opportunities offered in nursing from bedside nursing to telehealth and everything in-between. I feel like it will give me a good opportunity to grow throughout my career.
  2. What is the best skill that you feel you would bring to our organization?
    3.
    • I feel that my time management and organizational skills would be an asset to the unit. I have 10 years of experience working on time-sensitive software projects for clients that required me to coordinate efforts between multiple departments, third-party vendors, and clients for multiple ongoing projects.
  3. Are you comfortable working in a team environment?
    4.
    • I'm very comfortable working in either teams or in one-on-one environments. My previous job required collaboration between multiple departments including sales, it, and R & D  In order to complete projects in a timely manner. It also required extensive time working one-on-one with clients and working on my own analyzing and correcting data.
  4. What do you find to be the hardest part of nursing?
    5.
    • I think the hardest part of the job is dealing with the feelings of helplessness that can come up when you are caring for a patient who is close to the end of their life and there is nothing left that you can do to help them get better at that point. I saw a few patients in the ICU during clinical who did not have any hope of recovery and seeing the pain that their families had to go through was pretty difficult. I know it’s something that I will get used to with time.
  5. How have you dealt with an unhappy patient in the past?
    6.
    • To be completely honest I haven't had a situation with what I would consider to be an unhappy patient during my clinical experiences in nursing school so far. However, I can talk about a situation that came up for me as an implementation manager at a software company. I had just moved into the implementation Department from the company's help desk and was given a client who had been in implementation for over a year at that point. to give you a little context on an implementation, a typical one should take between 1 to 3 months depending on the product being ordered and the scope of the project. Needless to say the client was not very happy with our company at this point. After reviewing the project I contacted the client to talk about where we were at that point with the project oh, and where I thought mistakes had been made and what my plan was to move forward and get things back on track. I apologized to the client for what had happened so far and laid out a detailed plan with specific goals and a timeline included to get the project completed within 45 days. The client was initially skeptical which was understandable based on what they had gone through at that point but by the end of the project they were once again happy with the company and did not intend to leave us as they had previously stated.
